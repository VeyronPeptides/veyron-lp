RETA (RT3) — the specifics

Why is retatrutide called “GLP-3” when it isn't a GLP-3 receptor?

“GLP-3” is a field nickname, not a receptor name. It reflects the generational sequence the research community uses informally, GLP-1 (single-agonist), the dual-agonists as the second step, and retatrutide's triple-agonist mechanism as the third. The correct scientific designation is retatrutide, a GLP-1/GIP/glucagon triple-receptor agonist.

What makes the triple-agonist mechanism different from tirzepatide in the research?

Tirzepatide engages the GLP-1 and GIP receptors, two of the three pathways. Retatrutide adds simultaneous glucagon-receptor activation, which introduces an energy-expenditure component the dual-agonist mechanism does not include. In published trials, that third pathway is the variable researchers most often cite when explaining the gap in reported outcomes.

What it is

RETA (RT3)

Retatrutide is a single synthetic peptide engineered as a triple-hormone agonist, activating the GLP-1, GIP, and glucagon receptors simultaneously. Where first-generation research compounds engaged one pathway, and dual-agonists engaged two, this molecule was designed to hit all three at once. In the literature it is increasingly referenced as "GLP-3," a shorthand the field adopted to mark the generational jump.

Why researchers use it

The research community centers on retatrutide because the published figures broke from the expected curve. Study subjects in trials reported a ~24% mean reduction in body weight over 48 weeks, a magnitude some researchers openly compared to bariatric surgical outcomes rather than to earlier peptide classes.
